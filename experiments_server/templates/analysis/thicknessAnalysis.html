This analysis explores how strand thickness affects structural performance. All measurements are taken at 10% height compression for consistent comparison.

Weight Scaling Validation

Purpose: Validates simulation physics by confirming weight scales with material thickness.
Theory: Weight scales quadratically with material thickness (W ∝ t²) because increasing strand thickness increases the cross-sectional area (which is proportional to t²), and thus the volume and weight of material.
Expected: Data points should align with the theoretical quadratic curve.

Force Capacity Scaling Laws

Purpose: Identifies the dominant structural mechanics governing load capacity.
Theoretical Models:

  • Linear (F ∝ t): Pure axial compression failure
  • Quadratic (F ∝ t²): Bending-dominated failure
  • Quartic (F ∝ t⁴): Euler buckling from beam theory (Pcr = π²EI/(KL)², where I ∝ r⁴)

Interpretation: The theoretical curve that best fits experimental data reveals the primary failure mechanism.

Structural Efficiency Scaling

Purpose: Shows how efficiently structures support load relative to their weight as thickness increases.
Question Answered: Should you use thicker strands or more strands to maximize load per unit weight?
Theoretical Models:

  • Constant (E ∝ t⁰): Efficiency unchanged with thickness (axial-dominated)
  • Linear (E ∝ t): Efficiency increases linearly (bending-dominated, F ∝ t², W ∝ t)
  • Cubic (E ∝ t³): Efficiency increases rapidly (buckling-dominated, F ∝ t⁴, W ∝ t)

Interpretation: If data follows cubic scaling, thicker strands are significantly more weight-efficient than thinner ones.
